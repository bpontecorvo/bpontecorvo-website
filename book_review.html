I created this page to share some good and not-so-good books myself and my friends have been reading. I also just needed a good project for doing a bunch of web development from scratch. Enjoy.

Nudge 5/5
Intriguing Nobel Prize-winning economists explain how many annoying decisions could be made much more straightforward- choosing the right insurance plan, taxes, credit cards, and voting with simple “Nudges” and less bureaucracy.

Two world-renowned Behavioral Economists write about decision-making structures in the private and public sectors. How could we quickly help individuals make better choices when dealing with their taxes, insurance, mortgages, credit cards, organ donation, and climate change? Get rid of sludge, they say, and create choice architecture with Nudges and Libertarian Paternalism. It is written in a relaxed, subtle tone, with neither author taking themselves too seriously.

On Writing 2/5
This book is really gear towards aspiring fiction authors, or those wanting to sharpen their writing skillset. Less memoir, more manual, for serious writers only.

Stephen King lays out his process for story creation and writing, initial inspiration, demons, and tips and tricks for being a good writer. A beautifully woven book which is part memoir, part instruction. It begins at the early stages of his life, working as a teacher trying to get published, his first success, beating addiction, and getting hit by a van. You will learn how to build a writers “tool-box”, starting with simple rules like: “Omit needless words”, and "Never use passive voice".

Many of his best stories came from answering simple, horrid, hypotheticals: “What if a young mother and her son became trapped in their stalled car by a rabid dog” (Cujo. He does not mention many of my favorites such as Rage, It, The Shinning, Dr. Sleep, Long Walk, Green Mile, The Shawshank Redemption, & many others.

Endangered Orcas 5/5
A complete history and scientific analysis of the most well-studied (and loved) group of cetaceans - the Southern Resident Killer Whales. A must-read whale nerds & PNW buffs alike.

From the capture-era to present-day news, this book documents it all. It is interesting from a biology perspective, as well as just a human policy/interaction viewpoint. The SKRWs are the most well-understood group of Cetaceans on the planet- we know who everyone's parents, siblings, etc are, what they eat, who is born, and who dies… Yet, even in the most progressive parts of the wealthiest country on Earth, we are unable to enact change that will allow their population to rebound. Thoughtfully written and well researched, this book contains everything the layperson would/could want to know. While it is possible that they make a comeback, it is unlikely unless they start finding more Chinook Salmon very soon, or else the calves will keep dying. Some factoids that I learned while reading:

-Scientists accidentally killed a SRKW tagging it in 2016
-We still don’t know where they migrate in the Winter
-We do not know why Transients/Residents don't interact
-K27 Deadhead was named to honor the passing of Jerry Garcia (Deadhead also means floating log). Her first calf was named Ripple

Autobiography of Gucci Man 4/5
From poverty, violence, prison & drug addiction, to artistic genius, success & inventing a new genre of music, Radric Davis gives a raw, honest retelling of his life and the crazy shit that happened along the way.

I received this as a birthday gift from a friend. This book is never dull and reasonably well written; the author retells stories coherently while keeping his voice. It was fantastic to learn about his process for creating music, how he worked with his producer Zaytoven to make beats, and where he got his musical inspiration from. There were countless times throughout his life that Gucci easily could have died; it is frankly shocking that he made it out alive. It also made me wonder how many talented and young minds are killed only because they are products of the environments they grew up in. I would recommend this to anyone interested in Musicians, Trap Music, ATL, or learning more about the Black experience in the US.

The Tower 4/5
Tales of glory and deceit on Cerro Torre. A modern, engaging account for those who like reading about Argentina, climbing, or adventure.

Accomplished climber & author Kelly Cordes does his homework and puts together an enticing, captivating tale of the history that surrounded this mountain. He also positions the many controversial events in their historical context- for example Italy had suffered shame and defeat after WWII, and needed a hero (enter Cesare Maestri). Some "classic" mountaineering books are pretty archaic and have significantly boring sections- you will not find that here. The book is hardcover, has a nice font and spacing making it easy on the eyes, and has rad pictures sprinkled throughout. Go get yourself one.

Weather of the PNW 4/5
A comprehensive collection of PNW weather patterns, processes, factoids, and predictions about our climate in the next 50-100 years. Part meteorology 101, part PNW storm history, but all fun.

Written by a leading climate expert, this is digestible by both the expert and novice weather enthusiast, and you can skip over dull sections. Chapters cover everything from the basics, to floods, storms, coastal weather features, mountain-related phenomena, snowpack, forest fires, to climate change and weather prediction challenges. The author walks you through how meteorologists make forecasts, both historically and to this day. I learned that the weather in the PNW is some of the MOST PREDICTABLE in the nation. Sections are quite dry, but you can just skip them, and some graphics are shockingly low quality and hard to understand. Unlike his blog, you will find no politics here.

The Address Book 3/5
From unaddressed slums in India to some very offensively named lane in England, Diedre takes us on a global tour examining power and identity thru the lens of street names. A great read, but really for the geospatial community.

By examining different address systems worldwide, the author argues that addressing is one of the quickest ways to lift people out of poverty, get access to credit, vote, or receive Amazon packages. She also shows us how they can be used to oppress, leave out, and disconnect a population. What street do you live on, and how do you feel about the name?

Cascade Rock 3/5
Featuring old classics, new testpieces, and long alpine climbs, this is the best current guidebook for adventure rock climbs in WA State.

In addition to having great route descriptions, accurate topos, and great photos, this book brings to light new climbing areas that were preiously poorly documented. Places like the Payasten, Cascade Pass, Darrington and southwest BC Areas now have a modern guide to their best routes.

More: Seattle Public Library Guidebook Website

Crack Climbers Technique Manual 4/5
From basic fundamentals to complex off-width and chimney technique, this book covers it all with great diagrams

Technique is everything with crack climbing- without basics you will struggle on a 5.7. Kent Peace takes us from tips to full body spread chimney technique with detailed diagrams, subtle tips, and thorough descriptions. Overall quality is absolutely astounding and well worth the money if you aspire to improve as a crack climber. As a geophysical engineer and avid but average crack climber, the author does a great job documenting and breaking things down. Great photos as well.

More: Guidebook Website

Backcountry Ski and Snowboard Routes - WA 4/5
A lifetimes worth of old and new, easy and hard, half-day to week long fantastic backcountry tours.

This covers the best known routes in all areas of our state. Written by Martin Volkein and other experienced guides, the details are accurate, and breakdowns of each tour really give you an idea of the ski difficulty, skillset required, technical difficulty, and fitness required. If you are just getting into touring or are experienced rider, you will get great ideas of new places to go. Cool photos as well.

Hunter Gatherer's Guide to the 21st Century 2/5
An amazing first few chapters give way to unsubstantiated, ambigious, and unconnected ramblings. These exiled Evolutionary Biologists have some interesting perspectives on modern humans and our problems, but should have consulted an editor

Bret and Heather are passionate experts in arenas of trade-offs, adaptations, and other evolutionary things. They do make some interesting connections, and apply a biologists perspective to some modern problems. Their main problem, however, is waxing poetic with a broadstroke brush. It would have been cool if they could have focused and trimmed this work a bit. I did learn alot, but it could have been condensed into 50 pages. Maybe a biologist might like it more.
